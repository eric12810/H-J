Q & A

What does GDA stand for and what does it mean?

GDA stands for 'Guideline Daily Amounts'. They are a guide to how many calories and nutrients you should consume each day for a healthy, balanced diet â€“ so you can make more informed food choices. For more information on this and for other helpful healthy hints, please view our 'Healthy Leaflets'.

How do I know what my individual nutritional requirements are?

Nutritional requirements vary amongst each individual and are dependent on your age, gender, ethnic origin, activity levels, and occupation. It is possible to calculate individual requirements using scientific equations, however nutritional requirements are subject to change on a daily basis depending on what you do and the energy you exert. GDA values were established by a team of academic and industry experts to give people a guide about how much they should be consuming.

Why haven't you used traffic light labelling?

GDA is based on fact and does not demonise foods which traffic light labelling can do. Unlike traffic light labelling, GDA shows how a food will contribute to the overall diet, whereas traffic-light labelling only assesses each food per 100g without reference to the total diet or actual serving size. GDA labelling was chosen to be adopted as a Company as it was felt it was easier for consumers to understand and provides much more accessible, straightforward information compared to traffic light labelling.

Why has the menu been nutritionally analysed?

The Government have launched a 'Responsibility Deal' which is a voluntary scheme whereby food establishments can commit to providing nutritional information to their customers in order to help them make more informed food choices. Although it is a voluntary scheme at the moment, it is likely that in a few years it will become law for all food establishments to provide nutritional information about the food they offer.

How reliable is the information being supplied?

Nutritional information is based on representative values from manufacturers & producers, established industry-recognised databases and lab testing. Whilst ingredient information is based on standard product formulations, variations may occur due to difference in suppliers, ingredient substitutions, changes in recipe, product assembly at the time of production, the region of the country and season. It must be stressed that all food companies work to acceptable set tolerance levels when producing food products from set recipes, and H&J are no exception to this. All recipes are created bespoke by the onsite team and individually analysed using an industry recognised nutritional database. This is then compared to the dish in its raw and prepared form to ensure that it is an accurate reflection of the information given before publicising the nutritional information to the customer.

How do I know what an average serving size is?

In most cases the serving size explanation can be found at the bottom of each page of the menus. It is very difficult to quantify an average serving size as each person's own perception of a serving can vary considerably. We have created suggested serving sizes which you can follow as a guide to give you some idea about the content per portion.

Why have you not given any values for carbohydrates and protein?

We didn't want to overload people with information so we chose the top 5 components that we felt gave a representative nutritional snapshot of each food as well as following industry trends about the information being supplied. However, should you want more information about a particular dish, please ask and we can provide you with a full print-out report.

Who can I speak to if I have any questions concerning the nutritional information being provided?

For any queries, questions or quandaries please speak to the Catering Manager who should be able to deal with any inquiries you may have. Any questions that cannot be answered in house will be passed onto out Company Nutritionist.
